# Nuclear Infrastructure — Investment Thesis
*Second-layer / picks-and-shovels approach, drafted before any screener code*

## 1. Strategy Philosophy

Same framework as the hypersonic build: avoid the headline name everyone already owns, and avoid pure speculative bets on which specific technology wins. Instead, target the layer of suppliers, fuel providers, and infrastructure builders who get paid regardless of which reactor design, which utility, or which hyperscaler deal ultimately wins. Prime/headline exposure (CEG as "the nuclear utility stock," OKLO as "the SMR stock") is tracked but deliberately underweighted unless explicitly documented as an exception — the same rule that let PLTR into the hypersonic universe.

## 2. Why Nuclear, Why Now

The catalyst structure looks more like hypersonic than quantum: multi-year government and corporate contracts (NNSA enrichment funding, Navy reactor programs, 20-year hyperscaler power purchase agreements with Microsoft/Amazon/Google) rather than press-release-driven sentiment pops. Uranium supply has lagged a decade of underinvestment while AI data-center power demand has pulled forward utility interest in restarting and extending nuclear capacity. This is a capacity-and-fuel-cycle story, not a narrative story — closer to your risk comfort zone than quantum's IPO-wave volatility.

## 3. Exclusion Criteria

- No pure reactor-design bets as core holdings (tracked as documented exceptions only, capped position size).
- No legacy prime utilities included purely for nuclear optionality if nuclear is a small % of their generation mix.
- No tickers without verified active US-exchange listing and real trading volume (the MAXR/AJRD lesson from hypersonic applies here too).
- No double-counting names already carried in another theme's core book without an explicit decision on which theme owns the exposure.

## 4. Universe — Five Domains

**Domain 1 — Uranium Fuel Cycle (mining & enrichment)**
CCJ (Cameco, the established major fuel supplier), UEC (Uranium Energy), DNN (Denison Mines), LEU (Centrus Energy — sole Western HALEU enricher outside Russia; a genuine chokepoint position in the advanced-reactor fuel supply chain).

**Domain 2 — Reactor Components & Grid Infrastructure**
BWXT (reactor pressure vessels, naval and commercial nuclear components, HALEU/TRISO fuel manufacturing — the nuclear-sector equivalent of HWM/ATI in your hypersonic book), GEV (GE Vernova — turbines, grid equipment, and the BWRX-300 SMR design; flagged as a partial exposure, since wind and traditional gas turbine revenue heavily dilute the nuclear-specific signal). FLS (Flowserve) and IR (Ingersoll Rand) added as the flow-control/valve/pump layer — critical nuclear infrastructure that doesn't depend on which reactor design wins, and the replacement for CW's nuclear role now that CW stays solely in the hypersonic basket (see Section 6).

**Domain 3 — SMR / Advanced Reactor Pure-Plays (documented exception layer)**
OKLO (DOE-backed microreactor developer, pre-revenue, fission/fuel-recycling focus), SMR/NuScale (first NRC-approved SMR design, but down ~75% from highs — flagged as the highest-risk name in the universe; its structural position weakened further after the Utah project cancellation, so the screener needs a cash-burn filter on this ticker specifically, not just standard technicals). These sit here, not in Domain 2, precisely because they're betting on a specific design winning rather than supplying the whole sector.

**Domain 4 — Utilities Capturing the AI Power Premium**
CEG (Constellation — the benchmark hyperscaler power-deal name), VST (Vistra), TLN (Talen Energy). Included as a domain rather than excluded outright, since the AI-power-premium catalyst is real and measurable, but sized as the "landlord" layer rather than the core thesis.

**Domain 5 — Engineering, Procurement & Construction (EPC)**
J (Jacobs) and FLR (Fluor — which also holds a notable equity stake in NuScale, adding a direct structural tie to Domain 3). Modern nuclear deployment is as much a construction problem as a technology problem; the EPC layer captures the physical build-out that mining, components, and reactor design alone don't.

## 5. Watchlist (not yet promoted to active universe)

NXE, URG, EU, ISOU — earlier-stage uranium developers worth tracking for re-rating potential as supply tightens further.

## 6. Cross-Portfolio Overlap Check

- HWM and MTRN are already core holdings in your main space-infrastructure Robinhood book — nuclear exposure through those names is incidental, not additive, and shouldn't be double-counted as nuclear conviction.
- CW (Curtiss-Wright) stays solely in the hypersonic basket. Its nuclear-adjacent role (valves/pumps) is now covered by FLS and IR in Domain 2 instead, avoiding any dual-classification ambiguity.
- No current overlap between this universe and the hypersonic active 13, so the two books should diversify cleanly against each other.

## 7. Risk Factors

Uranium spot prices are volatile and sentiment-sensitive even though the long-term supply/demand case is fundamentals-driven. Several names here (CCJ, BWXT, GEV) are already richly valued off the 2025–2026 AI-nuclear narrative, so entries deserve more patience than the hypersonic build needed at its inception. SMR/microreactor names carry real execution and regulatory timeline risk — Oklo and NuScale are both pre-commercial-scale. Policy risk (NNSA funding, NRC licensing pace) can swing fast in either direction.
